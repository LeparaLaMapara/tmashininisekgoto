# Building a Data Science Team From Scratch: What I Actually Did

> This is not a management book chapter. It is the honest record of two years spent
> turning a small, undocumented, reactive data science team inside a large South African
> financial services group into one that ships. If you have just been handed a team like
> that, or you are about to build one, this is the order of operations I wish someone
> had given me.

## What I inherited

The handover was the worst I have ever received, because there was none.

There was no documentation of any kind. Models were running in production, with real
customers depending on them, and there was no record of how they were built, which data
sources they used, who consumed their outputs, or what their accuracy was. The products
existed. The knowledge about them lived nowhere.

The team was small and it had no head of data science, so it reported directly to a
business executive. That matters more than it sounds: there was nobody whose job it was
to translate between what the business asked for and what the team could sustainably
build, so everything was done on the fly.

The infrastructure was legacy. Deployments ran through
[Jenkins](https://www.jenkins.io) onto [Kubernetes](https://kubernetes.io) clusters
managed with [Rancher](https://www.rancher.com), a stack built for hosting applications,
not for data science. Nothing about it was distributed or scalable in the way data work
needs, and later we would feel that pain fully.

So: no map, no owner, no scale. Here is what I did about it, in the order I did it.

## Phase 1: Structure before anything

The team's first problem was not technical. It was that work had no shape. Things
started, stalled, and finished invisibly.

So the first thing I introduced was [agile](https://agilemanifesto.org) working through
[Jira](https://www.atlassian.com/software/jira). Two week sprints. A standup every two
days, not every day, because a small team drowning in production issues does not need
another daily meeting. At the end of each sprint we met, looked at what was done and
what was not, asked why, and unblocked each other.

I want to be honest about what this actually bought us, because it was not speed. It was
visibility. Within a few sprints you could see who knew what, which skills the team had
and lacked, and where work went to die. You cannot fix a team you cannot see.

## Phase 2: Write everything down

Once the work was visible, the next problem announced itself: everything important was
sitting in people's heads. If one person left, their models left with them.

So I introduced [Confluence](https://www.atlassian.com/software/confluence) as the
team's documentation home, and I seeded it myself with the things I had just suffered
through. The onboarding guide came first, written from my own painful experience of
joining: every tool we use, where to find it, how to get access, which entitlements to
request, and which regulations you must complete before you can work as a data
scientist, data engineer or machine learning engineer here. The vision was simple. The
team was going to grow, and growth must not depend on knowledge passed from mouth to
mouth.

Then came the hard part: we went into the backyard and reverse engineered our own
production models. Every model, every product the business was using, got documented:
what it does, how it works, what feeds it, who uses it. Nobody handed this to us. We dug
it out ourselves, because the alternative was operating systems we did not understand.

I documented the deployment process end to end, through Jenkins and Kubernetes, who owns
which step and what has to happen. I mapped the parties involved: who in technology we
talk to, and how. And where no formal process existed, we created one, including an SLA
document for business requests, so that requests stopped being favours done on the fly
and became tracked work with expectations on both sides. That was the beginning of
governance for us.

Documentation gets treated as admin. It is not. Writing down how everything works, when
nobody wrote it down for you, is a leadership act. It is the difference between a team
and a collection of individuals who happen to share a manager.

## Phase 3: The infrastructure reckoning

Then the infrastructure taught us the lesson I knew was coming.

During a platform migration, things broke, and one of our products started accumulating
a backlog of unprocessed records. The backlog grew past 200 million. The legacy stack
could not catch up, because it was never built for this: Jenkins and Kubernetes serve
applications beautifully, but they are not a distributed data processing platform. We
were paying the price of infrastructure chosen for a different job.

I looked around and made the case for [Databricks](https://www.databricks.com). And I
mean the whole case, end to end: getting the contract in place, standing up the
environment with the platform team, working through the networking, until we had a full
working environment. Then I built a migration framework to move us off the legacy stack:
a repeatable path, not a heroic one off.

The result made the argument better than any slide deck. Processing that would have
taken about two months on the old stack finished in a day. The backlog died. And the
team's mindset shifted with it: cloud first became the default assumption, and moving
our data storage to [AWS](https://aws.amazon.com) is now one of the team's biggest
running initiatives.

The lesson is not "buy Databricks". The lesson is that infrastructure must follow the
shape of the work. When your workload is distributed data processing and your platform
is an app server, no amount of team discipline fixes that mismatch.

## Phase 4: Teach relentlessly

A platform nobody can use is a very expensive way to feel modern.

The team had been working on laptops and virtual machines. Models lived on personal
machines where nobody else could reach them. [PySpark](https://spark.apache.org/docs/latest/api/python/)
and Databricks were new to almost everyone. So I taught, over and over: knowledge
sharing sessions on Databricks, on PySpark, on Jira, on Confluence, showing the value
rather than announcing the policy. People adopt tools when they see their own problem
get smaller, and slowly that happened: need something, it is on Confluence; need to
prototype, Databricks is faster than your laptop.

Then I made teaching a permanent institution: show and tells, every Friday. The rules
are loose on purpose. Share a model you are building. Share something you learned about
the business. Share a course you are studying, a better way to do something, or a piece
of technology news. The point is that everyone becomes able to do a bit of everyone
else's job, which is the only real insurance a small team has. Stakeholders started
attending and giving feedback, which quietly turned a team ritual into a business
relationship.

Alongside that, I pushed people toward the studies the company sponsors, because
personal development is not a perk. In a small specialised team it is the growth
strategy.

## Phase 5: Patterns, not heroics

By now the team could see its work, find its knowledge, and use real infrastructure.
The next gap was discipline: people were deploying by running jobs directly on
Databricks, by hand. It worked, but it was not reproducible, governed or accessible to
anyone but the person who did it.

So I introduced an MLOps and data engineering strategy layer for the team: this is how
we deploy here, these are the practices. Ingestion patterns. Model development patterns.
Dashboarding patterns on Databricks. A full [CI/CD](https://www.atlassian.com/continuous-delivery/continuous-integration)
path with governance, reproducibility and access control, instead of jobs run by hand.

And underneath all of it sat a piece of work I took on myself, which also became part of
my PhD research on distributed systems: I built an open source engine that lets you
describe a data or machine learning pipeline once, as a small folder of config and
Python, and run that same folder anywhere. That engine is
[Ubunye Engine](https://ubunye-ai-ecosystems.github.io/ubunye_engine/), and it became
the backbone of how the team does data engineering. I have written its whole story,
from [why convention is the real deliverable](/blog/ubunye-series-part1-why-convention)
to [how to use it with real examples](/blog/ubunye-series-part6-how-to-use-it), so I
will not repeat it here.

Rolling it out was not an email. It was onboarding, practical sessions, two to three
hands on hours every Friday, working through real pipelines together until the patterns
were the team's own.

## Phase 6: Learn to let go

Around this point I ran into my own ceiling: I was trying to be the scrum master and the
technical lead at the same time, and doing both badly on alternate days.

So I went to the executive and made the case for help. We brought in a project manager
and scrum masters to run the process. And we began handing some of the engineering
capabilities we had built over to the platform teams, whose job that properly is, so
they could support us instead of us carrying everything.

If you are building a team from scratch, this phase is not optional. Every structure you
personally hold together is a structure that fails when you are on leave. The goal was
never for me to run everything. It was for the team to run without me.

## What changed

Two years, measured plainly:

- A new model goes to production in under a week. It used to be unbounded.
- When a backlog builds up, we clear it in under a day. It used to take months and
  sometimes broke entirely.
- Onboarding is a documented path, not an oral tradition.
- Every production model is documented: what it does, what feeds it, who uses it.
- And the one that matters most: the team moved from fully reactive, always chasing the
  latest fire, to proactive for most of what we do.

## The order of operations, if you are starting from scratch

1. **Structure before tools.** Sprints and visible work first. You cannot fix what you
   cannot see, and no platform purchase fixes a team that cannot see its own work.
2. **Documentation is a leadership act.** Write the onboarding guide you wish you had
   received. Reverse engineer the systems nobody explained to you, and write those down
   too. Your team's knowledge should survive any single resignation, including yours.
3. **Infrastructure must follow the shape of the work.** App infrastructure for app
   workloads, distributed platforms for data workloads. The mismatch always sends the
   bill eventually, and ours arrived as 200 million unprocessed records.
4. **Teach relentlessly and in public.** Weekly show and tells, hands on sessions,
   sponsored studies. A small team where everyone can do a bit of everything is more
   resilient than a big team of specialists in silos.
5. **Patterns beat heroics.** Reusable ingestion, deployment and dashboarding patterns
   turn one person's competence into the team's default. That is also
   [why I build convention driven tools](/blog/ubunye-series-part1-why-convention).
6. **Hire out your bottlenecks, including yourself.** The moment you are both the scrum
   master and the lead, one of those jobs is being done badly. Ask for the project
   manager. Hand the platform work to the platform teams.
7. **Measure before and after.** Months to a day. Unbounded to under a week. Reactive to
   proactive. Numbers end arguments that adjectives never will.

None of this required genius. It required doing unglamorous things in the right order
and refusing to skip the boring ones. That is most of what building a capability from
scratch actually is.
