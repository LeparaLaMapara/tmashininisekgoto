# Building a Data Science Team From Scratch: A Real Life Roadmap

> This is not a management book chapter. It is the honest record of two years spent
> turning a small, undocumented, reactive data science team inside a large South African
> financial services group into one that ships. I have written it as a roadmap, because
> that is what I needed and could not find: not what good looks like, but what to do
> first, under real constraints, when nothing is good yet.

## The box I was in

Every roadmap is shaped by its constraints, so here were mine. Yours will look similar.

- **A team of fewer than five people**, carrying production systems that real customers
  used every day.
- **No handover.** The worst I have ever received, because there was none. No
  documentation existed about which models were running, how they were built, which data
  they used, who consumed them, or what their accuracy was.
- **No mandate.** The team had no head of data science, so it reported directly to a
  business executive. Nobody's job was to translate between what the business asked for
  and what the team could sustainably build, so everything was done on the fly.
- **Legacy infrastructure owned by other teams.** Deployments ran through
  [Jenkins](https://www.jenkins.io) onto [Kubernetes](https://kubernetes.io) clusters
  managed with [Rancher](https://www.rancher.com), a stack built for hosting
  applications, not for distributed data work.
- **A regulated environment.** Slow procurement, formal entitlements, real governance
  obligations. You do not simply install things here.
- **No extra budget on day one.** Not for platforms, not for training, and initially
  not for people.

If most of that list sounds like your situation, good. Here is the map.

## Phase 1: Structure before anything

**The constraint.** A handful of people drowning in production issues, with work that
started, stalled, and finished invisibly. I could not see the team, and the team could
not see itself.

**The move.** I introduced [agile](https://agilemanifesto.org) working through
[Jira](https://www.atlassian.com/software/jira). Two week sprints. A standup every two
days instead of every day, because a small team fighting fires does not need another
daily meeting. At the end of every sprint we looked at what was done and what was not,
asked why, and unblocked each other.

**What it cost.** Meeting fatigue at first, and some patience while people learned to
work in tickets instead of in their heads. The cheapest phase on the whole map. It costs
discipline, not money.

**The signal to move on.** Within a few sprints I could see who knew what, which skills
the team had and lacked, and where work went to die. When your problem changes from "I
cannot see the work" to "I can see the work and it all lives in people's heads", phase
one is done. For us that was roughly the first three months.

## Phase 2: Write everything down

**The constraint.** Everything important sat in individual heads. With fewer than five
people, one resignation could have taken a production system's entire knowledge with it.
And I already knew the team would grow, because I was asking for heads, so whatever we
knew had to stop being an oral tradition.

**The move.** I introduced [Confluence](https://www.atlassian.com/software/confluence)
and seeded it myself with the things I had just suffered through. The onboarding guide
came first, written from my own painful joining: every tool, where to find it, how to
get access, which entitlements to request, which regulations to complete before you can
work here as a data scientist, data engineer or machine learning engineer.

Then the hard part: we went into the backyard and reverse engineered our own production
models. Every model the business used got documented: what it does, how it works, what
feeds it, who uses it. Nobody handed this to us. We dug it out, because the alternative
was operating systems we did not understand.

I documented the deployment process end to end, mapped who in technology we talk to and
how, and where no formal process existed we created one, including an SLA document for
business requests. Requests stopped being favours done on the fly and became tracked
work with expectations on both sides. That was the start of governance.

**What it cost.** My own evenings, honestly. Documentation is unglamorous and nobody
asks for it. I treated it as a leadership act anyway: writing the guide you wish you had
received is the difference between a team and a collection of individuals who share a
manager.

**The signal to move on.** People started answering their own questions from Confluence
instead of interrupting each other. And the real payoff came later: when we eventually
added two new heads, they onboarded from a documented path, not from mouth to mouth.

## Phase 3: The infrastructure reckoning

**The constraint.** A regulated group's procurement process, no infrastructure budget of
my own, and a legacy stack owned by other teams. I could not simply buy a platform. And
the workload was fundamentally mismatched: Jenkins and Kubernetes serve applications
beautifully, but they are not a distributed data processing platform.

**The move.** I prepared the case for [Databricks](https://www.databricks.com) early,
and reality made the argument for me. During a platform migration things broke, and one
of our products began accumulating unprocessed records. The backlog passed 200 million.
The legacy stack could never catch up.

Here is the roadmap lesson, and it is the most transferable thing on this page: **you do
not get budget for infrastructure, you get budget for pain.** When the incident came, I
did not bring a complaint to the executive. I brought a costed proposal that was already
prepared. Then I carried it end to end: the contract, the environment with the platform
team, the networking, and a migration framework off the legacy stack. A repeatable path,
not a heroic one off.

**What it cost.** Months of patience while the case waited for its moment, and personal
risk, because I owned the outcome. If the platform had failed, that was mine.

**The signal to move on.** Processing that would have taken about two months on the old
stack finished in a day. The backlog died, nobody argued about cloud strategy again, and
cloud first became the default. Moving our data storage to
[AWS](https://aws.amazon.com) is now one of the team's biggest running initiatives.

## Phase 4: Teach the team you have

**The constraint.** I could not hire my way to the new stack. The team I had was hired
for a different world: models lived on laptops and virtual machines where nobody else
could reach them, and [PySpark](https://spark.apache.org/docs/latest/api/python/) and
Databricks were new to almost everyone. My training budget was zero.

**The move.** I taught, over and over. Knowledge sharing sessions on Databricks, on
PySpark, on Jira, on Confluence, always showing the value rather than announcing the
policy. People adopt tools when they see their own problem get smaller: need something,
it is on Confluence; need to prototype, Databricks beats your laptop.

Then I made it a permanent institution: show and tells, every Friday. The rules are
loose on purpose. Share a model you are building, something you learned about the
business, a course you are studying, a better way to do something, or a piece of tech
news. The zero budget training programme, in other words. Stakeholders started attending
and giving feedback, which quietly turned a team ritual into a business relationship.
Alongside it I pushed people toward the studies the company sponsors, because in a team
this small, personal development is not a perk. It is the growth strategy.

**What it cost.** A few hours every Friday, protected fiercely, and the repetition tax:
teaching the same thing five times until it sticks.

**The signal to move on.** Models stopped living on personal machines. When the team
reaches for the shared platform without being told, this phase has worked.

## Phase 5: Patterns, not heroics

**The constraint.** The team could now use real infrastructure, but everyone deployed by
hand, running jobs directly on Databricks. It worked, and it was not reproducible,
governed, or accessible to anyone but the person who did it. In a regulated environment
that is not a style problem. It is a risk.

**The move.** I introduced an MLOps and data engineering strategy layer: this is how we
deploy here. Ingestion patterns. Model development patterns. Dashboarding patterns. A
full [CI/CD](https://www.atlassian.com/continuous-delivery/continuous-integration) path
with governance, reproducibility and access control, instead of jobs run by hand.

Underneath it sat work I took on myself, which also became part of my PhD research on
distributed systems: an open source engine that lets you describe a data or machine
learning pipeline once, as a small folder of config and Python, and run that same folder
anywhere. That engine is
[Ubunye Engine](https://ubunye-ai-ecosystems.github.io/ubunye_engine/), and it became
the backbone of how the team does data engineering. Its whole story is written up, from
[why convention is the real deliverable](/blog/ubunye-series-part1-why-convention) to
[how to use it with real examples](/blog/ubunye-series-part6-how-to-use-it).

Rolling it out was not an email. It was onboarding, practical sessions, two to three
hands on hours every Friday, working through real pipelines together until the patterns
were the team's own.

**What it cost.** The engine cost me nights and weekends, which is why it doubled as PhD
work, and the Friday sessions cost the team real delivery hours in the short term.

**The signal to move on.** A deployment stopped depending on who did it. Any pattern
that survives its author is working.

## Phase 6: Learn to let go

**The constraint.** Me. I was trying to be the scrum master and the technical lead at
the same time, and doing both badly on alternate days. Every structure I personally held
together was a structure that failed when I was on leave.

**The move.** I went to the executive and made the case for help. We added two heads to
the team. We brought in a project manager and scrum masters to run the process. And we
began handing some of the engineering capabilities we had built over to the platform
teams, whose job that properly is, so they support us instead of us carrying everything.

**What it cost.** Ego, mostly. Letting go of things you built and run well is harder
than building them.

**The signal.** The team runs without me in the room. That was the goal the whole time.

## What two years actually bought

- A new model goes to production in under a week. It used to be unbounded.
- When a backlog builds up, we clear it in under a day. It used to take months and
  sometimes broke entirely.
- Two new team members onboarded from a documented path, not an oral tradition.
- Every production model is documented: what it does, what feeds it, who uses it.
- And the one that matters most: the team moved from fully reactive, always chasing the
  latest fire, to proactive for most of what we do.

## The roadmap on one page

1. **Structure before tools.** Sprints and visible work first. You cannot fix what you
   cannot see, and no platform purchase fixes a team that cannot see its own work.
2. **Documentation is a leadership act.** Write the onboarding guide you wish you had
   received. Reverse engineer the systems nobody explained to you and write those down
   too. Your team's knowledge should survive any single resignation, including yours.
3. **You get budget for pain, not for infrastructure.** Prepare the proposal before the
   incident, because the incident is coming. When it arrives, bring a costed plan, not a
   complaint, and be willing to own the outcome personally.
4. **Teach the team you have.** You will not always be allowed to hire. A weekly show
   and tell is a training programme with a budget of zero, and it makes a small team
   resilient because everyone can do a bit of everyone's job.
5. **Patterns beat heroics.** Reusable ingestion, deployment and dashboarding patterns
   turn one person's competence into the team's default. That is also
   [why I build convention driven tools](/blog/ubunye-series-part1-why-convention).
6. **Hire out your bottlenecks, including yourself.** The moment you are both the scrum
   master and the lead, one of those jobs is being done badly. Ask for the project
   manager. Hand the platform work to the platform teams.
7. **Measure before and after.** Months to a day. Unbounded to under a week. Reactive to
   proactive. Numbers end arguments that adjectives never will.

None of this required genius. It required doing unglamorous things in the right order,
under constraints that never fully went away, and refusing to skip the boring ones. That
is most of what building a capability from scratch actually is.
